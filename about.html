OUR STORY

I'm a programmer in a large part because of my father. He was not a programmer, he was a school principal, a second generation teacher who wanted us to get into something other than teaching.

His school got a Commodore VIC-20 computer and he wanted me to try it out.I didn't really do too much programming with the VIC-20 (it only had 5K of available RAM) but what little programming I did, I enjoyed.

One day my grandfather, a retired vice principal and school inspector, had a math problem for my father, along these lines: "A farmer was selling cows for $5, pigs for $3, and chickens two for a dollar.

He went to the market and sold 100 animals for $100. How many of each kind of animal did he sell? "My father wasn't really interested in solving the question.

It was the summer, and since nobody else wanted it, I had the school's VIC-20 at home. I said, "How about I write a program to solve that?"

I wrote a brute force "algorithm" and that poor VIC-20 took a few minutes to solve the problem, but it did.That's when I realized computers could solve real life problems and it enhanced my interest.

(Funnily enough I saw the same math problem recently, and, true to form, didn't do any mathematical reasoning, just wrote a tiny scrap of Common Lisp that solved the problem sub-second, for old times' sake.

I eventually wanted my own computer; I saved up for a while and bought a Tandy Color Computer 2 and really worked hard at learning BASIC and assembly language on it. I got OS/9 so I could learn C. I went and got a programming diploma, which included Basic, COBOL, and just enough C to do printf and scanf. At home I used C to replace OS/9's horrifically bad text editor with a horrifically bad text editor of my own that I at least liked better.
